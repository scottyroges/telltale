# Insights as Biographer Notes

## The Biographer's Challenge

A professional biographer conducting interviews for someone's life story faces a unique set of challenges:
- Hundreds of hours of conversation producing thousands of data points
- Need to track what's been covered and what gaps remain
- Must balance following interesting tangents with comprehensive coverage
- Need to identify the most important moments, themes, and turning points
- Must decide in real-time what to dig deeper on vs. what to move past

How do they do this? And how can our "insights" system learn from their practices?

## How Professional Biographers Organize Notes

### 1. Multi-Dimensional Organization

Real interviewers don't just store chronological transcripts. They organize notes along multiple axes:

**By Theme/Topic:**
- Childhood & family background
- Education & formative experiences
- Career & major accomplishments
- Relationships & personal life
- Pivotal moments & turning points
- Values & beliefs
- Regrets & lessons learned

**By Source:**
- Which interview session
- Date and context
- What prompted this story (the question asked, or tangent followed)
- Physical/emotional state of the subject

**By Type:**
- Direct quotes (verbatim)
- Paraphrased stories
- Observed reactions (subject got quiet, lit up, avoided topic)
- Contradictions with earlier statements
- Follow-up questions to ask
- Cross-references to other stories

### 2. Importance Markers

Not all notes are equal. Biographers mark:
- **Core stories** - the ones that define who this person is
- **Emotional moments** - where the subject showed strong feeling
- **Gaps** - areas that need more depth
- **Contradictions** - inconsistencies to resolve
- **Connectors** - stories that link different life phases

### 3. The "Heat Map" Mental Model

Experienced interviewers develop a mental map of the territory:
- Areas they've covered deeply (hot)
- Areas they've touched on briefly (warm)
- Areas completely unexplored (cold)
- Areas the subject avoids (blocked)

This helps them balance breadth and depth.

## How They Stay On Track

### The Narrative Arc Framework

Biographers work with a rough structure in mind:
1. **Origins** - where did this person come from?
2. **Formation** - what shaped who they became?
3. **Calling** - what drove them forward?
4. **Challenges** - what obstacles did they face?
5. **Transformation** - how did they change?
6. **Legacy** - what matters most to them now?

Each interview session maps progress against this framework.

### The Coverage Checklist

Before each session, they review:
- What chapters/sections need more material
- Which important people haven't been discussed enough
- Which time periods are thin
- Which themes are underdeveloped

### The "Completeness" Sense

For any topic, they track:
- **Surface covered** - basic facts known
- **Depth achieved** - understand the emotions, motivations, context
- **Connections made** - how this links to other parts of their life
- **Significance understood** - why this matters to the person's story

## How They Decide What to Ask Next

### 1. Follow the Energy

The most important signal: **What makes the subject come alive?**
- When do their eyes light up?
- When do they slow down and choose words carefully?
- When do they speak quickly, unable to get the words out fast enough?
- When do they become quiet or evasive?

These emotional markers point to what matters most.

### 2. Fill the Gaps

A biographer's job is comprehensive. They actively track:
- **Time gaps** - "We've talked about your 20s and 40s, but not your 30s"
- **Topic gaps** - "We haven't discussed your relationship with your father"
- **Depth gaps** - "I know you started that company, but why did you really do it?"

### 3. Follow the Threads

Stories naturally connect. Good interviewers notice:
- Repeated themes across different stories
- People who appear in multiple contexts
- Values demonstrated through actions
- Contradictions that reveal complexity

When a thread appears, they pull on it: "You mentioned that feeling before when talking about X. Does that connect to this?"

### 4. Chase the Turning Points

Biographies are built on pivotal moments:
- Major decisions and what drove them
- Moments of crisis or transformation
- Times when the person could have gone a different direction
- The "before" and "after" of significant events

These deserve deep exploration.

### 5. Understand the Why

Facts are easy. Understanding is hard. They keep asking:
- "Why did you make that choice?"
- "What were you feeling at that moment?"
- "Looking back, what do you make of that now?"
- "What did you learn from that experience?"

## Applying This to Our Insights System

### Current State
Right now, insights are:
- Flat list per book
- Basic text extraction from conversations
- No organization or prioritization
- No tracking of coverage or gaps
- No guidance on what to explore next

### What If We Built What Biographers Need?

**1. Thematic Organization**
- Auto-tag insights by topic/theme
- Show heat map of which themes are well-covered vs. sparse
- Suggest under-explored areas

**2. Importance Scoring**
- Mark pivotal moments, emotional peaks, core stories
- Surface the "this is who I am" moments
- Identify contradictions or unresolved threads

**3. Coverage Tracking**
- Visual representation of life phases covered
- Track people, places, themes mentioned
- Highlight gaps in the narrative

**4. Connection Mapping**
- Link related insights across conversations
- Show recurring themes or people
- Surface contradictions to resolve

**5. Session Preparation**
- Before each conversation: "Here's what's under-explored"
- Surface follow-up questions from previous sessions
- Suggest threads worth pulling

**6. The "Next Best Question" Engine**
- Given the corpus of insights, what's most important to ask next?
- Balance breadth (coverage) with depth (significance)
- Follow energy (what has the subject engaged with most)

## Design Principles

If we rebuild insights with this biographer's mindset:

1. **Structure over chaos** - Insights need organization, not just chronological storage
2. **Importance over volume** - Not all notes are equal; surface what matters
3. **Gaps as first-class data** - What's missing is as important as what's captured
4. **Connections over isolation** - Insights gain meaning through relationships
5. **Guidance over observation** - The system should help decide what to ask next
6. **Evolution over snapshots** - Understanding deepens over time; insights should reflect that

## Questions to Explore

1. How do we automatically identify "pivotal moments" vs. routine stories?
2. Can we detect when a subject is emotionally engaged vs. going through the motions?
3. How do we build a "coverage map" of someone's life story?
4. What's the right way to organize insights - by theme, by time, by importance, or all three?
5. How do we help the AI interviewer develop the "sense" for what to ask next?
6. Should insights be mutable (refined over time) or immutable (preserving the raw record)?

## Next Steps

This is exploratory thinking. To make it real, we'd need to:
1. Study how real biographers work (read about their process)
2. Prototype a richer insight structure
3. Experiment with auto-tagging and importance scoring
4. Build tools to visualize coverage and gaps
5. Test with real interview sessions - does it actually help?
