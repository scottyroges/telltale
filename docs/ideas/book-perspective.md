# Book Perspective: First Person vs. Third Person

## The Core Question

When someone comes to Telltale to create their life story, we need to ask: **Whose voice tells this story?**

- **First Person**: "I was born in Chicago in 1952. I remember..."
- **Third Person**: "She was born in Chicago in 1952. She would later recall..."

This isn't just a stylistic choice. The perspective fundamentally changes:
- What inputs we need from the customer
- How we conduct interviews
- What the final book feels like to readers
- Who the book is really for

## Understanding the Options

### First Person: The Subject as Narrator

**What it is:**
The subject tells their own story directly. The reader experiences life through their eyes.

**What it feels like:**
> "I never expected to see her again. When she walked into that café twenty years later, my hands started shaking. All those feelings I thought I'd buried came rushing back..."

**Strengths:**
- Intimate and immediate
- Authentic voice
- Reader connects directly with the subject
- Natural for personal stories
- Subject owns their narrative

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to subject's perspective
- Can't easily include context they don't know
- Harder to be objective about themselves
- May feel awkward analyzing their own choices
- Voice needs to be consistent throughout

**Who it's for:**
- Gift to children/grandchildren ("hear Grandma's voice")
- Personal legacy ("this is how I saw my life")
- People who want their authentic voice preserved

### Third Person: An Objective Narrator

**What it is:**
Someone else tells the story about the subject. Creates distance and perspective.

**What it feels like:**
> "She didn't expect to see him again. When he walked into that café twenty years later, the life she'd built seemed suddenly fragile. Everything she'd convinced herself was true began to crack..."

**Strengths:**
- Can provide outside context and perspective
- Can analyze choices and patterns
- Can include things subject didn't know at the time
- Can shift between time periods smoothly
- Can interpret meaning more freely

**Weaknesses:**
- Less intimate
- Loses the subject's authentic voice
- Can feel more distant or formal
- May not capture how they really felt

**Who it's for:**
- Books meant to be "about" someone (parent, grandparent)
- Stories where context and analysis matter
- People uncomfortable speaking in first person
- Books that need objectivity

### Hybrid: The Best of Both?

**What it is:**
Mix first person narrative with third person context or analysis.

**What it feels like:**
> "I never expected to see her again."
>
> This was 1989, three years after the divorce. He'd built a new life in Portland, remarried, had a daughter. The past was supposed to stay past.
>
> "When she walked into that café, I felt like I was twenty-five again..."

**Strengths:**
- Preserves authentic voice for key moments
- Allows context and analysis
- Flexible and dynamic
- Can shift perspective when needed

**Weaknesses:**
- Can feel disjointed if not done well
- Requires skill to blend smoothly
- May confuse readers about who's speaking

## How Perspective Changes the Process

### For First Person Books

**Interview Focus:**
- Capture HOW they tell stories, not just what happened
- Pay attention to their voice, phrases, patterns
- Ask for internal thoughts and feelings heavily
- Get them to narrate scenes: "What did you see? What were you thinking?"
- Record turns of phrase, expressions, verbal tics

**What We Need Beyond Interviews:**
Honestly? Maybe not much. The interviews ARE the raw material. We're refining their voice, not creating a new narrator.

**Writing Challenge:**
- Maintain consistent voice throughout
- Make it sound like them, not generic
- Handle time transitions naturally ("Looking back now..." vs. "At the time, I thought...")
- Stay in their knowledge/perspective (can't describe what they didn't know)

**Special Considerations:**
- Some people are naturally good storytellers; others aren't. Do we polish their voice or preserve it as-is?
- How do we handle uncomfortable truths? First person requires owning your choices.
- Voice consistency: interviews happen across weeks/months. How do we unify the voice?

### For Third Person Books

**Interview Focus:**
- Focus on facts and events
- Still need emotions, but less concerned with exact phrasing
- Can ask analytical questions: "Why do you think you made that choice?"
- Can ask hypotheticals: "What would your mother have thought?"
- Less pressure on them to "perform" the story

**What We Need Beyond Interviews:**
- Historical/cultural context they may not have
- Understanding of events they didn't witness
- Perspective on how others saw them
- Analysis of patterns they can't see themselves
- Timeline and fact-checking

**Writing Challenge:**
- Create a narrative voice (whose voice is it?)
- Balance subject's perspective with outside view
- Include context without overwhelming the story
- Interpret their life without misrepresenting it

**Special Considerations:**
- Who is the narrator? Omniscient? Close third person following them?
- How much do we analyze vs. just report?
- Can we include things they don't want to say about themselves?

## What This Means for Product Design

### Option 1: We Choose (Default First Person)

**Approach:** Everyone gets first person memoirs. It's our format.

**Pros:**
- Simplest for customers (no decision paralysis)
- Optimizes our process for one format
- Clear value prop: "Your voice, your story"
- Most authentic feeling

**Cons:**
- Some stories work better in third person
- May not fit all customers' needs
- Limits our market

### Option 2: Customer Chooses Upfront

**Approach:** During onboarding, ask: "Do you want to tell your story in your own voice (I) or have us write it about you (she/he)?"

**Pros:**
- Gives customers control
- Can optimize interviews for their choice
- Serves different needs

**Cons:**
- Most customers won't understand the difference
- Decision paralysis early in the process
- Complicates our interview process
- May need different pricing (third person requires more work)

### Option 3: We Choose Based on Content

**Approach:** After initial interviews, our system recommends a perspective based on their style, content, goals.

**Pros:**
- Informed decision
- Customers see examples first
- We can guide them to what works best

**Cons:**
- Delayed decision
- What if we're wrong?
- Harder to pivot mid-process

### Option 4: Hybrid Is Default

**Approach:** Use first person for scenes and stories, third person for transitions and context. Best of both.

**Pros:**
- Most flexible
- Works for all types of stories
- Professional memoir style

**Cons:**
- Hardest to execute well
- May feel inconsistent if not done skillfully
- Customers may find it confusing

## The Interview Implications

### Does Perspective Change What We Ask?

**Questions that work for both:**
- "Tell me about your childhood."
- "What was your mother like?"
- "How did you meet your spouse?"
- "What was the hardest decision you ever made?"

**Questions more important for first person:**
- "How do you usually describe this to people?"
- "What phrase do you always use when talking about this?"
- "Walk me through that moment - what did you see, hear, feel?"
- "What were you thinking right then?"

**Questions more important for third person:**
- "Why do you think you made that choice?" (asking them to analyze)
- "What would others have said about you at that time?"
- "What didn't you know then that you know now?"
- "Looking at it objectively, what patterns do you see?"

### Does It Change Interview Depth?

**For First Person:**
We need the same depth of content, but we ALSO need:
- Their narrative voice and style
- How they naturally tell stories
- Their verbal patterns and expressions
- Consistent tone across topics

**For Third Person:**
We can be more focused on content:
- Facts, events, timeline
- Emotions and motivations (but less concerned with exact words)
- Analysis and reflection
- Context and meaning

## The Technical Question

**How does this affect our AI interviewer?**

**For First Person:**
- Need to learn and mimic their voice
- Maintain character consistency
- Encourage detailed scene-setting
- Capture quotes and phrases
- Help them "show not tell"

**For Third Person:**
- Focus on extracting information
- Can be more analytical in questions
- Less concerned with their exact phrasing
- Can probe for objective details
- Can ask them to step outside themselves

**For Both:**
The conversational interview process works either way. The difference is mainly in what we capture and how we use it.

## Recommendation: Start Simple, Stay Flexible

**Initial Approach:**
1. **Default to first person** for V1
   - Clearest value prop: "Your story in your own words"
   - Simplest to explain and execute
   - Most emotionally resonant

2. **Design interviews to capture both**
   - Get their voice AND the facts
   - Ask both subjective and analytical questions
   - Record how they tell stories and what they tell

3. **Build flexibility into the writing pipeline**
   - Don't lock into perspective too early
   - Could offer both versions
   - Could let customers switch after seeing samples

4. **Gather data on what works**
   - Do customers prefer first person?
   - Are some stories better in third?
   - Does it vary by age, culture, story type?

**Future Evolution:**
- Offer choice once we understand preferences
- Maybe different tiers (first person standard, third person premium?)
- Hybrid as advanced option
- Let customers see samples in both perspectives before choosing

## Open Questions

1. Do customers intuitively understand the difference without seeing examples?
2. Does perspective affect willingness to share difficult stories?
3. Should family history books use different perspective than personal memoirs?
4. Can our AI write convincingly in first person? Does it feel authentic?
5. Do readers (grandkids, etc.) prefer one perspective over another?
6. How does perspective affect the emotional impact and value of the book?

## Conclusion

Perspective matters more than it might seem. It's not just grammar - it's about:
- **Intimacy** (first person) vs. **Clarity** (third person)
- **Voice** (theirs) vs. **Narrative** (ours)
- **Preservation** (how they talk) vs. **Polish** (professional writing)
- **Gift** (hear Grandma) vs. **Legacy** (learn about Grandma)

For V1, first person seems like the right default - it's more unique, more personal, harder to get elsewhere. But we should design our process to stay flexible as we learn what customers really want.
